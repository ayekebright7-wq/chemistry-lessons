Lesson 3

Factors Affecting Solubility

Temperature, pressure, and the nature of substances

25 minutes Intermediate Level WASSCE Topic: Factors Affecting Solubility

Teacher's Explanation

"Have you noticed sugar dissolves faster in hot tea than in cold water? Or that you can dissolve more salt in hot water? And why do fizzy drinks lose their fizz when warm? This lesson explores the three main factors that control solubility."

Ghanaian Market Example

Soda Drinks at the Market

When you buy a cold Fanta from the fridge at a Ghanaian shop, it's very fizzy. But if you leave it in the sun, it goes flat quickly. Why? Because cold liquids can hold more carbon dioxide gas. Also, the sealed bottle keeps the gas dissolved under pressure.

The Three Main Factors

Temperature

Affects how much solute can dissolve.

Effects:
  • For solids: Higher temperature = More solubility
  • For gases: Higher temperature = Less solubility
  • Ghanaian example: More sugar dissolves in hot porridge

Pressure

Only affects gases in liquids.

Effects:
  • Higher pressure = More gas dissolves
  • Lower pressure = Gas escapes
  • Example: Soda bottles are sealed under pressure

Nature of Substances

Based on polarity (from Lesson 2).

Remember:
  • Polar dissolves polar
  • Non-polar dissolves non-polar
  • Example: Salt (polar) dissolves in water (polar)

Temperature Effect Demonstration

Hot Water

Many solute particles dissolved

Cold Water

Fewer solute particles dissolved

Pressure Effect on Gases

High Pressure (Sealed Bottle)

✓ More CO₂ dissolved in soda

✓ Very fizzy when opened

Low Pressure (Opened Bottle)

✗ CO₂ gas escapes

✗ Drink goes flat

Lesson Recap

Key Points to Remember:

  • Temperature: ↑ for solids, ↓ for gases
  • Pressure: Only affects gases - more pressure = more gas dissolves
  • Nature: Polarity determines if substances mix
  • Different rules for solids vs gases!

WASSCE Tip

Always specify whether you're talking about solids or gases when discussing temperature effects. Many students lose marks by saying "solubility increases with temperature" without specifying which type of solute.
