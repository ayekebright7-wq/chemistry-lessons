Lesson 4

Saturated, Unsaturated & Supersaturated Solutions

Understanding different types of solutions and solubility curves

20 minutes Intermediate Level WASSCE Topic: Types of Solutions

Teacher's Explanation

"Imagine adding sugar to tea. At first it all dissolves (unsaturated). Keep adding and eventually some sugar stays at the bottom (saturated). But there's a tricky third type we can create in the lab - supersaturated solutions that are unstable!"

Making Sugar Syrup

Making Ghanaian Sugar Syrup

When making sugar syrup for sobolo, you can dissolve a lot of sugar in hot water. As it cools, the solution becomes supersaturated. If you add a sugar crystal (seed), all the excess sugar crystallizes at once - this is how rock candy is made!

Three Types of Solutions

Unsaturated

Can dissolve more solute

All solute particles are dissolved

Clear solution, no solid at bottom

Example: Tea with little sugar

Saturated

Maximum solute dissolved

Dissolution rate = crystallization rate

Solid may be visible at bottom

Example: Tea with max sugar

Supersaturated

More solute than normal

Unstable - will crystallize suddenly

Created by cooling saturated solution

Example: Rock candy solution

Remember: A saturated solution at a higher temperature becomes supersaturated when cooled slowly without disturbance. Adding a seed crystal causes instant crystallization!

How to Read a Solubility Curve

  • X-axis: Temperature (°C)
  • Y-axis: Solubility (grams solute/100g water)
  • Below the curve: Unsaturated solution
  • On the curve: Saturated solution
  • Above the curve: Supersaturated solution

Lesson Recap

Key Points to Remember:

  • Unsaturated: "Room for more" solute
  • Saturated: "Completely full" - equilibrium state
  • Supersaturated: "Overfull" - unstable and will crystallize
  • Solubility curves show how much dissolves at different temperatures

WASSCE Tip

When describing saturation, always mention that in a saturated solution, the rate of dissolution equals the rate of crystallization. This shows you understand dynamic equilibrium.
